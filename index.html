What is Stoicism?

Stoicism is an ancient Greek philosophy that teaches us to live in harmony with nature and reason, cultivating inner peace through self‑discipline and virtue. The early Stoics believed that the goal of life is to live in agreement with nature and that virtue is the only true good. Humans are unique in the animal kingdom because of our rationality, and it is through the proper use of reason that we achieve a flourishing life. Throughout Roman times, Stoicism inspired thinkers like Seneca, Epictetus and Emperor Marcus Aurelius.

Living in Agreement with Nature

Click to learn how Stoics saw Nature as a rational, ordered cosmos.

The Stoics held that the universe is a well‑ordered system governed by divine reason or logos. Living in agreement with nature means aligning your will with the rational order of the cosmos and accepting that events are fated. By understanding our place in the universe and that everything happens for a reason, we cultivate tranquility and resilience.

Virtue Is the Only Good

Click to learn why Stoics believed that virtue alone is sufficient for happiness.

According to Stoicism, external things such as wealth, health, and reputation are indifferent: they are neither good nor bad in themselves. Virtue—acting according to reason and fulfilling one’s duties—is the only thing that truly matters and is both necessary and sufficient for happiness.

Transforming the Passions

Click to see how Stoics managed emotions and transformed passions.

The Stoics classified irrational emotions or passions into four types—distress, fear, appetite, and pleasure—and taught that these disturbances arise from misguided judgments. Instead of suppressing feelings, the sage cultivates three ‘good states’: joy, caution and wish, which are rational and aligned with nature.

The Four Cardinal Virtues

Stoic ethics teaches that wisdom, justice, courage and moderation are unified virtues. To possess one fully is to embody them all.

Wisdom

Good sense, intelligence and practical reason.

Justice

Fairness, honesty and doing right by others.

Courage

Fortitude in adversity and facing fears.

Temperance

Self‑control and moderation in all things.

Philosophers & History

Explore an interactive timeline of Stoicism’s founders and key figures.

c. 300 BCE

Zeno of Citium

Zeno founded the Stoic school around 300 BCE in Athens. He taught on the Stoa Poikile and emphasized living according to nature.

c. 280 BCE

Cleanthes

Succeeding Zeno, Cleanthes led the school and composed hymns to Zeus.

c. 230 BCE

Chrysippus

Chrysippus, the third head of the Stoa, systematized Stoic logic and ethics. Despite his prolific writings, only fragments remain.

c. 50 CE

Seneca

A Roman statesman and playwright, Seneca popularized Stoicism in his letters and essays and advised Nero. He reminded us that “We suffer more often in imagination than in reality”.

c. 90 CE

Epictetus

Born a slave, Epictetus taught that while we cannot control external events, we can control how we respond. He was expelled from Rome and taught in Nicopolis, influencing later Stoics.

c. 170 CE

Marcus Aurelius

Emperor of Rome from 161–180 CE, Marcus Aurelius penned his Meditations as private notes to himself. His writings remain a timeless guide to inner strength and virtue.
